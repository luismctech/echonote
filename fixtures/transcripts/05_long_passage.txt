The principle of locality is one of the most important ideas in modern computer architecture. Programs tend to access a relatively small portion of their memory at any given time, and they tend to reuse data and instructions that were recently accessed. This observation drives the design of caches at every level of the memory hierarchy. Spatial locality means that if a particular memory location is referenced, nearby locations are likely to be referenced soon. Temporal locality means that the same location is likely to be referenced again in the near future.
